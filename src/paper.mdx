## Overview

Model-based reinforcement learning agents learn a world model and then practice inside it. This works well when useful experiences are already in the replay buffer, but it becomes slow when rewards sit behind rare bottlenecks.

**Mind Dreamer** asks a simple question: if the world model already understands the shape of the environment, why should imagination always begin from places the agent has physically visited?

Instead of only replaying history, Mind Dreamer generates plausible latent starting points near promising frontiers. These generated anchors let the agent practice around bottlenecks before random exploration happens to find them.

## The Problem

Latent imagination gives model-based agents a powerful internal simulator, but the simulator is usually tethered to the replay buffer. Rollouts begin from states the policy has already visited, so the agent can keep practicing the familiar parts of the world while missing the transition that actually matters.

Mind Dreamer calls this **Historical Tethering**. The world model may already know enough local geometry to suggest where a bridge, doorway, or maneuver could exist, but the policy still waits for on-policy data to arrive there.

## Mind Dreamer

Mind Dreamer turns the world model from a passive replay machine into an active curriculum generator.

The key design is restraint: Mind Dreamer does not ask the model to hallucinate arbitrary futures. It searches for short, plausible jumps that make the agent's imagination less local without abandoning the environment's learned structure.

## Takeaways

1. **Imagination should not be limited to replay.** A learned world model can suggest plausible places to practice before the policy reaches them through exploration.

2. **Generated anchors act like a curriculum.** They focus imagined rollouts on regions that are useful, uncertain, and close enough to the learned manifold.

3. **The gains are strongest when discovery is hard.** Sparse rewards and narrow transition regions are where historical tethering hurts most.

4. **The technical details are in the paper.** This page highlights the intuition; the full derivation and ablations are available in the paper and archive links above.
